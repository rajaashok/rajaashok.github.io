Proxy Servers: Two Roles and Three Modes

Ask ten engineers to explain a proxy and you will get ten diagrams that all look the same: a box in the middle with arrows on both sides. The box is real. The confusion is that most people mix up what the proxy represents with how it operates — and once you separate those two questions, every proxy on the internet snaps into place.

The core idea

Stop mixing roles with modes

A proxy is just an intermediary that receives a request, does something useful with it, and passes it along. The interesting questions are not whether there is a middleman, but two others: who does it stand in front of, and how visible and honest is it about being there.

The first question gives you the role. A forward proxy stands in front of the clients and represents them on the way out. A reverse proxy stands in front of the servers and represents them on the way in. That is the entire distinction, and it is architectural: it is decided by where the proxy sits in the topology, not by any feature it happens to run.

The second question gives you the mode. The same forward proxy can be open to the public or private, can intercept traffic transparently or require configuration, and can hide the client’s identity to different degrees. Those are operating choices layered on top of the role — not separate species of proxy.

The two roles

Who does the proxy represent?

Forward and reverse are mirror images. In both, a client, a proxy, an internet hop, and a set of servers appear. What changes is which end the proxy is bolted to — and that single fact flips almost everything else.

Forward · represents the client

Forward proxy

Sits between users and the wider internet, on the client side of the conversation. Outbound requests hit it first. Because it speaks on the client’s behalf, it can control, filter, and cache what leaves the network, and it can hide who the original client was from the destination.

Typical jobs: corporate access control and content filtering, egress logging and compliance, caching frequently fetched external resources, and web scraping through rotating egress addresses.

Examples: Squid, Blue Coat ProxySG, and the outbound proxies baked into most corporate networks.

Reverse · fronts the servers

Reverse proxy

Sits between the internet and a fleet of backend servers, on the server side. Inbound requests reach it last, just before a server would. Because it speaks for the servers, it decides which backend handles each request and shields the fleet from the public.

Typical jobs: load balancing across backends, TLS termination, response caching, rate limiting, DDoS absorption, canary and blue-green routing, and URL or content rewriting.

Examples: NGINX, HAProxy, Envoy, and every CDN edge and API gateway you have ever used.

The memory hook

A forward proxy is grouped with the clients; a reverse proxy is grouped with the servers. Draw a dotted box around each and the boundary makes the role obvious: forward = client-side box, reverse = server-side box. If the proxy protects and represents the people making requests, it is forward. If it protects and represents the machines answering them, it is reverse.

Same picture, mirrored

Trace one request through each role

The clearest way to feel the difference is to follow a single request and notice where the proxy sits in the path. In the forward case it is the first hop; in the reverse case it is the last hop before a server.

  1. Forward — the client hits the proxy first. A user’s request leaves their machine and immediately reaches the forward proxy, which applies access rules, caching, and identity control before the request ever touches the public internet.
  2. Forward — then it steps out to the world. The proxy makes the outbound call on the client’s behalf, retrieves the resource from the destination server, and returns the response back down to the user. The destination mostly sees the proxy, not the client.
  3. Reverse — the request crosses the internet first. A user’s request travels across the public network toward your service and arrives at the reverse proxy, which is the public face of the system. No backend is exposed directly.
  4. Reverse — it does its job, then picks a backend. The reverse proxy terminates TLS, enforces limits, checks its cache, then load-balances the request to a healthy server, and streams the response home. The client mostly sees the proxy, not the fleet.

The three modes

How a proxy operates, layered on top of its role

Once the role is fixed, three operating choices describe the behavior. These are not new kinds of proxy — they are settings a forward proxy (usually) can be run in. Confusing them with roles is the single most common mistake.

01

Open

A forward proxy that anyone can use, with no private access required.

  • Publicly reachable, which makes it convenient for quick tests and one-off access to the open web.
  • Operated by a stranger — so it can log, inspect, or modify the traffic that passes through it.
  • Treat it as hostile infrastructure. Never send credentials, tokens, or sensitive data through an open proxy.
02

Transparent

Intercepts traffic without any configuration on the client.

  • The client is not told to use a proxy; the network silently routes traffic through it — common on campus, office, and ISP networks.
  • Used for filtering, caching, and policy enforcement at the network boundary.
  • “Transparent” means no client setup, not undetectable. Its presence can usually still be observed by inspecting headers and behavior.
03

Anonymity spectrum

Controls what the destination learns about your IP and about the proxy itself.

  • Anonymous: hides your real IP, but reveals that a proxy is in use.
  • Distorting: reveals that a proxy is in use, and supplies a false client IP.
  • High-anonymity (elite): hides your real IP and hides the obvious signals that a proxy is present at all.
Precision matters here

A proxy usually changes the apparent source IP and may alter headers. It does not “rewrite your identity,” and anonymity is a spectrum, not a switch. What the destination learns depends entirely on which anonymity level the proxy runs at — and an open proxy in front of you may quietly downgrade all of it.

The myth worth breaking

A proxy is not a VPN

This is the confusion that costs people the most, so it deserves its own line. A proxy and a VPN both put an intermediary between you and a destination, but they operate at different layers and make different promises.

Scope

A proxy typically routes a single application’s traffic — often just your browser or one tool that is configured to use it. A VPN captures traffic for the whole device at the network layer.

Encryption

A proxy does not automatically encrypt all of your traffic. A VPN builds an encrypted tunnel from your device to the VPN server by design. Assuming a proxy encrypts everything is how sensitive data leaks.

Intent

Proxies are usually about control, caching, routing, and appearing to come from somewhere else. VPNs are usually about confidentiality and a trusted network boundary for the entire device.

Say it plainly

Proxy ≠ VPN. If your goal is “encrypt everything my laptop sends,” a proxy is the wrong tool. If your goal is “route this one app through a controlled, cached, or different-looking path,” a proxy is exactly right.

Choosing well

Which one do you actually need?

Roles and modes are not interchangeable, so the decision is really two decisions: pick the role from where you sit, then pick the mode from how you must behave.

Reach for a forward proxy when

  • You control the clients and need to govern what leaves the network.
  • You want egress logging, filtering, or compliance at the boundary.
  • You are caching external resources many clients fetch repeatedly.
  • You need requests to appear to originate from a controlled set of addresses.

Reach for a reverse proxy when

  • You run the servers and need to hide and balance the fleet.
  • You want TLS termination, response caching, and rate limiting in one place.
  • You need DDoS absorption and a single hardened public surface.
  • You want safe rollout controls like canary, blue-green, and rewriting.

The judgment layer

Why this matters beyond the definition

A model that answers “whose side, and how honest” is not just interview trivia. It is the lens you use to review a configuration — including one an assistant generated for you.

An AI coding assistant will happily write you an NGINX config, a Squid rule set, or a cloud load-balancer template in seconds. The question is whether it put the proxy on the right side of the topology, chose an operating mode that matches your privacy and security needs, and did not quietly promise encryption a proxy cannot provide. That judgment does not come from memorizing five proxy names. It comes from holding the two-question model in your head: whose side is this proxy on, and how is it allowed to behave?

The takeaway

Two roles — forward represents the client, reverse fronts the servers. Three modes — open, transparent, and the anonymity spectrum. One caveat — a proxy is not a VPN. Carry those three sentences and you will never draw the wrong box again.
