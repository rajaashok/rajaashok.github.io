Don't Memorize 300 Problems.
Memorize This Decision Tree.

The interview isn't a memory test. It's a classification test — and classification is learnable. One tree, six branches, told as the story of how interviews are actually won.

RAJA · JULY 2026 · MEGA POST · KEEP THIS TAB OPEN

In 2016, I bombed an interview I should have aced.

I'd prepped for weeks. Grinded a few hundred problems, notebook full of solutions, confidence full of helium. Then the interviewer shared a problem I'd never seen, and my brain did something cruel: it started flipping through every solution I'd memorized, like a card catalog on fire. Was this like the islands one? The intervals one? Three minutes of silence. He typed something. It wasn't praise.

Driving home, the diagnosis arrived, obvious and a little insulting: I had memorized answers in a game about recognizing questions. The problem he gave me was a sliding window wearing a costume — and I owned three sliding window solutions that I couldn't see through the disguise.

Years later, sitting on the other side of the table, I watch candidates make my exact mistake every hiring season. The strong ones aren't the ones who've seen everything. They're the ones who can classify anything — because nearly every interview problem is one of about 25 patterns in a costume, and classification takes a minute once you have the tree.

So here's the tree. The full map is on the first tab — screenshot it, print it, tape it over your desk. Then each tab walks one branch like a story: what the trigger words are, why the trick works, where the bodies are buried, and just enough Python to make it stick.

The map I wish someone had handed me in 2016

Now, a confession about how this tree gets used badly: people treat it like an answer key. It isn't. It's a conversation script. The winning move is to walk it out loud — "okay, sorted array, looking for a pair… that smells like two pointers or binary search" — because the interviewer is grading your navigation, not just your destination.

The 60-second ritual: read the problem twice. Name the data structure out loud. Walk that branch of the tree out loud. Propose the pattern and its complexity — then touch the keyboard. Every senior engineer you admire does some version of this. It just doesn't look like a ritual anymore because it's fast.

Each tab above is one branch of this tree, drawn in full and walked through slowly — with stories from both sides of the interview table, and just enough Python to make the tricks stick.

You're not learning 300 solutions. You're learning 25 locks and the shape of each key.

Arrays & strings — the ninety-second tell

I've interviewed a lot of engineers over the years, and here's a secret from my side of the table: I can usually tell within ninety seconds whether a candidate has a system or a stack of memorized solutions. The tell isn't speed. It's the first sentence they say after reading the problem.

Candidates with a system say things like "unsorted array, need pairs that sum to a target — hash map, one pass." Candidates without one say "hmm, I think I've seen this…" and start excavating.

This branch is where that system pays off most, because arrays are 40% of everything you'll face. Here's the full branch:

The array / string branch · walk top to bottom

FIRST QUESTIONDo I need to find things fast?

The top of the branch is the HashMap, and it's the top for a reason: the most common transformation in all of interviewing is turning "search the array again and again" — O(n²) — into "file everything in labeled drawers, check the drawer" — O(n). Two Sum, the most famous interview question on the planet, is exactly this: for each number, check the drawer for its complement before filing the current number. (That order matters. File first and a 4 will happily pair with itself to make 8. I've watched that bug eat fifteen minutes of a strong candidate's interview.)

REP: Two Sum · Group Anagrams · Longest Consecutive Sequence

SORTED? SAY IT OUT LOUDThe word "sorted" is a gift

If the input is sorted and you don't say "binary search or two pointers" within ten seconds, you've left points on the table. Sorted input means every comparison kills half the search space (Binary Search, O(log n)) or tells you exactly which end to move (Two Pointers — sum too small? left finger moves right; too big? right finger moves left). It's the number-guessing game from childhood — "higher… lower…" — a billion numbers, thirty guesses.

REP: Binary Search · Two Sum II · 3Sum · Container With Most Water

THE CATERPILLAR"Contiguous subarray" means sliding window

Any problem that says contiguous subarray or substring is asking you to check a stretch of consecutive elements — and the naive way is to check all O(n²) stretches. The Sliding Window is a caterpillar instead: the head crawls forward every step, and the tail only catches up when the window breaks a rule. Every element enters once and leaves once. Here it is finding the longest substring without repeats:

def longest_unique(s):
    seen = {}                 # char -> last index we saw it
    left = best = 0
    for right, ch in enumerate(s):
        if ch in seen and seen[ch] >= left:
            left = seen[ch] + 1        # tail catches up
        seen[ch] = right
        best = max(best, right - left + 1)
    return best                        # one pass. O(n).

Read the middle line again — left = seen[ch] + 1. That's the entire pattern: the tail jumping forward past the duplicate. Everything else is bookkeeping.

REP: Longest Substring Without Repeats · Minimum Window Substring · Max Consecutive Ones III

MIDDLE OF THE BRANCHFour specialists worth knowing cold

Heap for anything "top K" — you don't sort the whole leaderboard to find the podium; you keep a K-sized VIP room where the weakest member guards the door (that's why it's a min-heap for "K largest" — the counterintuitive detail interviewers love to probe). Prefix Sum for range-sum questions — mile markers on a highway; any stretch is just marker[j] − marker[i]. Monotonic Stack for "next greater element" — the one pattern on this branch that nobody invents live in 45 minutes, so learn it before you need it. And Merge Intervals for anything with meetings or bookings: sort by start time, sweep once, and overlaps can only ever be between neighbors.

REP: Kth Largest · Subarray Sum Equals K · Daily Temperatures · Meeting Rooms II

BOTTOM OF THE BRANCHThe move that never embarrasses you

And when nothing above fires? Two honorable exits. One: ask "would this be easy if the array were sorted?" — sorting costs O(n log n) and unlocks half the branch above. Two: say, with a straight face, "let me brute-force it first, then optimize." I have never once penalized a candidate for that sentence. I have penalized plenty for fifteen minutes of silence while they hunted for the clever answer.

Linked lists — five moves, endlessly recombined

A story from the other side of the table. A candidate once told me, mid-problem: "I hate linked lists." I asked why. "Because I can't see them."

That's actually the correct diagnosis — and the fix is the whole branch. You can't index into a linked list, you can't glance at it, so every technique is really a way of seeing without looking: clever pointers that report back where things are. There are only five moves. Every "hard" list problem is choreography built from them.

The linked list branch

MOVE ONEThe tortoise and the hare

Two runners on a track, one walking, one sprinting double speed. If the track loops, the sprinter must lap the walker — that's cycle detection with zero extra memory. If it doesn't loop, the sprinter hits the wall exactly when the walker reaches halfway — that's middle-finding without ever counting nodes. One trick, two superpowers:

def middle(head):
    slow = fast = head
    while fast and fast.next:          # chant: fast AND fast.next
        slow, fast = slow.next, fast.next.next
    return slow    # fast hit the end; slow is standing halfway

That while fast and fast.next is a chant. Say it every time. Nearly every linked-list crash in interview history is someone advancing fast.next.next when fast.next was already None.

REP: Linked List Cycle · Middle of the List · Happy Number

MOVE TWOReversal, rehearsed until boring

In-place reversal is four lines that everyone fumbles under pressure, which is precisely why you rehearse it until it's muscle memory: carry three sticky notes — prev, curr, next — save next, flip curr's arrow backward, shuffle all three forward. The one commandment: save next before you flip, or the rest of the list floats away into garbage collection and takes your offer with it.

REP: Reverse Linked List · Reverse Linked List II · Reverse Nodes in K-Group

MOVES THREE & FOURThe commute trick and the checkout lines

Where do two lists intersect? The two-pointer switch: when your pointer finishes its own list, teleport it to the head of the other one. Both pointers travel exactly n + m steps, so they arrive at the junction in perfect sync — two people walking each other's commutes, meeting where the routes merge. It feels like a magic trick; it's just equalized distance. And merging K sorted lists? A min-heap holding one head per list — K checkout lines, one bagger, always grabbing from whichever line has the smallest front item. O(N log k), and the interviewer will absolutely ask why not merge one-by-one (that's O(N·k) — say it before they ask).

REP: Intersection of Two Lists · Merge K Sorted Lists

THE BOSS FIGHTReverse + merge

Palindrome check. Reorder list. These look advanced, and they're actually a combo meal: fast & slow to find the middle, in-place reversal on the back half, then walk the two halves together. If you drilled moves one and two, the boss fight is free. And if you're ever truly stuck on a list problem — draw five boxes and arrows on paper and push your pointers by hand. Every list bug ever written is a pointer aimed somewhere its author didn't draw.

REP: Palindrome Linked List · Reorder List

Trees — one fork decides everything

My favorite moment to watch in an interview is the first ten seconds after a tree problem lands, because there's exactly one decision that matters and candidates either know it or don't: am I going wide, or am I going deep?

Everything with the word "level" in it goes wide — that's BFS with a queue. Everything about paths, depths, and ancestors goes deep — that's DFS with recursion. Answer that fork and you've done half the problem before writing a character.

The tree branch · wide or deep?

GOING WIDEBFS and the snapshot trick

Level-order traversal is a queue and a loop, but the detail that separates working code from scrambled output is the size snapshot: at the top of each round, freeze the queue's current length — that count is exactly one level. Process that many, enqueue their children, repeat. Skip the snapshot and levels bleed into each other silently; you won't notice until your output is wrong and the clock is ugly.

REP: Level Order Traversal · Right Side View · Zigzag Traversal

GOING DEEPThe one-sentence religion of DFS

Here's the entire philosophy of tree recursion: ask each child for its answer, combine, pass it up. Depth is 1 + the taller child. Diameter is best-left-path plus best-right-path. Path sums, balance checks, subtree matches — all the same sentence with a different combine step.

def depth(node):
    if not node:                       # the base case IS the design
        return 0
    return 1 + max(depth(node.left), depth(node.right))

The discipline that makes it work: don't trace the recursion. Ever. Trying to simulate the whole call stack in your head is how people melt down mid-interview. Instead: define what the function returns for any subtree, nail the null base case, write the combine. Trust that it works on children — because by induction, it does.

REP: Maximum Depth · Diameter of Binary Tree · Path Sum II

THE FAMILY REUNIONLowest common ancestor

LCA sounds intimidating and is actually one elegant observation: recurse into both subtrees looking for either target, and the first node that finds one on its left and one on its right is the meeting point — the first person both cousins report up to at the family reunion. One subtlety worth saying out loud: a node counts as its own ancestor, so if you find one target while searching, you can stop — the other is somewhere below it.

REP: LCA of a Binary Tree · LCA of a BST (easier — use the ordering!)

THE BST FACTWorth an actual tattoo

One fact unlocks every BST question: inorder traversal visits values in sorted order. Validate a BST? Check that your inorder sequence strictly increases. Kth smallest? Stop at the Kth visit. (The classic trap: validating by comparing only parents to children — a grandchild can violate its grandparent's range and your check passes anyway. Pass down (min, max) bounds, or lean on inorder.) And for serializing a tree to a string, go pre-order with explicit null markers — root arrives before its subtrees, so rebuilding is a simple recursive read. Skip the null markers to "save space" and the shape becomes ambiguous — flat-pack furniture with no instruction sheet.

REP: Validate BST · Kth Smallest in BST · Serialize & Deserialize

Graphs — the costume department

Graph problems almost never say "graph." They say cities and flights. Courses and prerequisites. Employees and managers. Islands in a grid of water. The problem-writers are running a costume department, and your first job — before any algorithm — is to undress the problem: say out loud what your nodes are and what your edges are.

I've watched candidates solve the hardest part of a graph problem in one sentence — "okay, each course is a node, each prerequisite is a directed edge" — and then coast. I've also watched candidates skip that sentence and flail for half an hour at a problem they actually knew. Model first. Then this branch picks the algorithm for you:

The graph branch · six questions, six algorithms

THE STONE IN THE PONDBFS owns unweighted shortest paths

When every step costs the same — and a maze or grid always qualifies — BFS explores in rings, like ripples from a stone. The first time you touch the target is provably the shortest route, because you reached it in the smallest possible number of rings. The template is worth having cold:

from collections import deque

def shortest_steps(grid, start, goal):
    q, seen = deque([(start, 0)]), {start}
    while q:
        (r, c), d = q.popleft()
        if (r, c) == goal:
            return d                   # first arrival = shortest
        for dr, dc in ((1,0),(-1,0),(0,1),(0,-1)):
            nxt = (r + dr, c + dc)
            if nxt not in seen and open_cell(grid, nxt):
                seen.add(nxt)          # mark on PUSH, not on pop
                q.append((nxt, d + 1))
    return -1

The comment on the second-to-last line is the classic trap, so it gets its own paragraph: mark nodes visited when you push them, not when you pop them. Mark-on-pop lets the same cell enter the queue five times from five neighbors, and your "shortest" path quietly isn't.

REP: Shortest Path in Binary Matrix · Word Ladder · Rotting Oranges

WHEN EDGES HAVE PRICESThe shortest-path ladder

Weighted edges, none negative? Dijkstra — BFS's older sibling, where the queue becomes a min-heap keyed on total cost, and you always expand the cheapest frontier. The greedy bet works for one reason: with no negative edges, a settled node can never be undercut later. Break that assumption with negative weights and you climb down to Bellman-Ford — slower at O(V·E), but unbreakable: relax every edge V−1 times, and if a V-th sweep still improves something, congratulations, you've found a negative cycle (in interview-land: a free-money loop, and usually the actual question). Need every pair's distance on a small graph? Floyd-Warshall — three nested loops, K outermost, five lines total.

REP: Network Delay Time · Cheapest Flights Within K Stops · Find the City

FRIEND CIRCLES & GETTING DRESSEDThe two non-path questions

Not every graph question is about paths. "Are these connected? How many islands / provinces / clusters?" — that's Union-Find: everyone starts alone, every edge merges two circles, and two nodes are connected exactly when they report to the same root. With path compression it's effectively O(1) per operation, which is why it wins whenever edges arrive as a stream. And "what order do I take the courses?" — that's Topological Sort, the getting-dressed algorithm: socks before shoes, shirt before jacket. Kahn's version is mechanical — repeatedly take anything with zero unmet prerequisites and cross it off — with a built-in bonus: if you finish without processing every node, the leftovers form a cycle. Course Schedule, both parts, one algorithm.

REP: Number of Provinces · Accounts Merge · Course Schedule I & II

The 10-second graph chant: unweighted → BFS. Weighted, no negatives → Dijkstra. Negatives → Bellman-Ford. All pairs → Floyd-Warshall. "Connected?" → Union-Find. "What order?" → Topological sort. Six lines. Say them in the shower until they say themselves.

DP & greedy — the branch with the worst PR

Let me tell you how "dynamic programming" got its name, because it explains a lot. Richard Bellman, working at RAND in the 1950s, needed a name that his funding-averse boss couldn't object to — so he picked two words that meant almost nothing and sounded impressive. Seventy years later, students are still intimidated by a name chosen specifically to be meaningless.

Strip the name away and DP is one household idea: this big problem contains the same small problem many times — so solve each small one once and write the answer down. You already do this. It's called not re-deriving your times tables.

The DP branch · three questions

START HERE, ALWAYSMemoization is recursion plus a notebook

Write the honest recursive solution first — the slow one, the one that just states the truth of the problem. Then hand it a notebook: before computing, check the notebook; after computing, write it down. Watch what that does to Fibonacci:

def fib(n):                            # honest. and 2^n slow.
    return n if n < 2 else fib(n-1) + fib(n-2)

from functools import cache

@cache                                 # same function. n calls.
def fib(n):
    return n if n < 2 else fib(n-1) + fib(n-2)

Same function. One decorator. Exponential becomes linear, because the notebook collapses a tree of repeated calls into n distinct ones. That's the entire top-down method — and in Python, @cache makes it nearly free. The one real bug in memoization: caching on a key that doesn't capture everything that varies between calls. Miss one parameter and you'll serve stale answers with total confidence.

REP: Climbing Stairs · House Robber · Longest Common Subsequence

THE ONE-SENTENCE TESTTabulation, or: fill the table forward

Bottom-up is the same idea walked in the opposite direction — start from the base cases and fill a table toward the answer. No recursion, no stack limits, and often the table shrinks to two variables once you notice each row only reads the previous one. But here's my hard rule, learned from watching dozens of DP meltdowns: you may not write a loop until you can say the sentence "dp[i] means ___" in plain English. If you can't say the sentence, the loops are guesswork and the bugs are unfindable. If you can, the code writes itself.

REP: Coin Change · Unique Paths · Word Break

THE SEDUCTIVE COUSINGreedy — but prove it

Greedy means never looking back: grab the best local option and commit. When it's right (activity selection, jump game) it's gloriously short. When it's wrong, it fails silently and confidently — my favorite demonstration is coin change with coins {1, 3, 4} making 6: greedy grabs 4+1+1 (three coins) and struts right past 3+3 (two). So the professional habit is the counterexample hunt: before committing to greedy, spend thirty seconds actively trying to break your own rule, out loud. Interviewers score the attempt to falsify yourself higher than the algorithm.

REP: Jump Game · Non-overlapping Intervals · Gas Station

The DP litmus test: overlapping subproblems and optimal answers built from optimal sub-answers? Two yeses → DP. One no → it's backtracking or greedy wearing DP's jacket. Don't force the table onto problems that merely look hard.

Everything else — the specialists

Every toolbox has a drawer of tools you rarely reach for, but when you need them, nothing else works. That's this tab. Four patterns with trigger words so specific that recognizing the trigger basically is the solution — plus the escape hatch for when nothing fits at all.

The specialists branch

THE NEON SIGN"Prefix" means Trie

A Trie is a tree where each root-to-node path spells a prefix, one character per hop — so every word sharing a prefix shares a path. That's why autocomplete-style queries are instant while a HashMap (which hashes whole strings) can't help at all. The word "prefix" in a problem is a neon sign; walk toward it. One classic slip: forgetting the end-of-word flag — "app" and "apple" share a path, and without the flag you can't tell which stops are real words.

REP: Implement Trie · Word Search II · Design Add & Search Words

THE CARD TRICKXOR and the vanishing pairs

XOR has one magical property — a number XORed with itself vanishes — and one famous consequence:

def single_number(nums):
    x = 0
    for n in nums:
        x ^= n         # pairs annihilate: a ^ a == 0
    return x           # the loner survives

Every pair annihilates; the loner survives. Five lines that feel like a card trick, O(1) space, and the interviewer smiles because you clearly know the classic. Resist the urge to memorize twenty bit hacks — you need three: XOR cancellation, n & (n-1) drops the lowest set bit, and shifts multiply or divide by two.

REP: Single Number · Missing Number · Counting Bits

THE SEESAWTwo heaps hold the median

"Median of a data stream" sounds like it needs sorting on every insert. Instead: a max-heap holds the smaller half, a min-heap holds the larger half, sizes kept within one. The median is always sitting on top of one heap (or averaged across both tops) — a seesaw where you only ever look at the two kids nearest the middle. Rebalance after every insert; let the heaps drift and the seesaw lies to you.

REP: Find Median from Data Stream · Sliding Window Median

SOMETIMES IT'S JUST MATHCounting problems in disguise

Unique paths through an m×n grid is a beloved DP exercise — and also, quietly, one binomial coefficient: of your m+n−2 total moves, choose which ones go down. C(m+n−2, m−1). Done, O(1). When the answer depends on how many ways rather than which ways, check for a formula before building a table. (Then watch for overflow in factorials — compute nCr multiplicatively, and say why.)

REP: Unique Paths (formula version) · Pow(x, n)

THE ESCAPE HATCHReframe — the most senior move on this page

And when nothing fits? Change the question. Can't solve "minimum removals"? Solve "maximum keeps" — same problem, friendlier shape. Stuck on a weird string puzzle? Model states and transitions, and suddenly it's a graph. Problem-writers deliberately dress familiar patterns in unfamiliar stories; reframing is how you undress them. Do it out loud — "let me restate this" — because the restatement scores points before you've solved anything. And here's the rep that compounds fastest of everything in this post: for every problem you fail, write one sentence. "This was actually ___ in disguise." Thirty of those sentences and you'll feel the tree growing behind your eyes.

80% of interviews start with this decision tree. The other 20% start with you staying calm because you have one.

Pattern recognition > code writing.

The tree won't write the code for you — it wins you the first sixty seconds, and the first sixty seconds set the tone for the whole interview. The same muscle carries over to product sense, system design, and every other blank-page moment in your career. That's the whole thesis behind hackproduct.com — think, choose, code, optimize.
